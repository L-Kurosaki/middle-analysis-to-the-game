Formation & Result

Every team's starting formation, matched to whether they won, drew, or lost. Filtered to formations used in 5+ matches for a fair read.

4-2-3-1 won 53.1% of its 32 matches — the best return of any formation used more than 5 times.
3-5-2 went winless across all 11 matches, conceding 2.27 goals/game on average — the worst defensive record in the tournament.
4-3-3 drew far more than it lost (12 draws vs 11 losses) with the tournament's best goals-against average (1.03) — a stable, low-risk shape rather than a high-win one.
02

Midfielder Contribution: On-Ball and Off-Ball

Split into two distinct roles, each split Win / Draw / Loss (n=49 / 30 / 49 team-matches): on-ball — do midfielders' passes actually progress the ball forward (10+ metres), not just circulate it — and off-ball — what share of the team's defensive actions (pressures, interceptions, blocks, tackles, recoveries) come from midfielders when the team doesn't have the ball.

On-ball: winning teams' midfielders contributed 28.8% of progressive passes vs. 27.0% for draws and 28.5% for losses — a real but small edge, not a decisive gap.
Off-ball: midfielders made up 37.1% of defensive actions in wins vs. 35.7% in losses — teams that win lean on midfielders defensively slightly more too.
Neither gap alone is large. That's the honest finding: no single midfield metric decides matches by itself — which is exactly why we combined them next.

Combined Composite Midfield Index

On-ball progression (40%) + off-ball defensive share (35%) + substitution effectiveness while losing (25%, corrected for circularity — see methodology) → one 0–100 score per team-match.

Wins average 51.5 vs. 49.9 for losses and 49.4 for draws — the composite correctly orders win > loss > draw, but the margin is narrow. Notably, several of the top-10 team-matches are famous upsets (Saudi Arabia beating Argentina, Cameroon beating Brazil, South Korea vs. Brazil) — teams that controlled midfield well but still lost or won as underdogs. That's a real limitation worth saying in the room: this index measures midfield performance, not the final scoreline.
03

Do Substitutions Actually Work?

Every substitution tagged with the score state at that moment, then compared: goals scored in the 15 minutes before vs. after, by why the sub was made.

Losing teams' scoring rate jumps 7x in the 15 minutes after a substitution (0.044 → 0.302 goals) — the clearest behavioral signal in the data.
Caveat worth saying out loud: this doesn't prove the sub caused it — losing teams naturally push forward late in games regardless. It's a correlation a coach should read as "consistent with subs helping," not proof.
Winning teams do the opposite by design — their scoring rate drops from 0.554 to 0.197 after a sub, consistent with subbing to protect a lead rather than extend it.

05

Finding: Descriptive Ability ≠ Predictive Power

Most sports-analytics projects report a correlation and stop. We took the extra step: built a proper rolling-form prediction test on a full league season (La Liga 2015/16, 380 matches, 20 teams) and asked directly — does any of this actually call the result before kickoff? Rolling averages use only each team's prior 5 matches, trained on the first 70% of the season and tested on the final 30% in time order, so nothing from the future leaks backward.

No — the midfield metrics don't predict games. 47.2% accuracy, AUC 0.476 — worse than just always guessing "home team wins" (50.9%, AUC 0.500 by definition). We tested it properly and it fails, cleanly.
But something else in the same data does. Recent form — points won in the last 5 matches — gets 58.5% accuracy and AUC 0.609, a real edge over baseline.
And combining them makes it worse (52.8%), not better — a clean, reportable example of a weak feature diluting a good one.
Why this is the finding, not a footnote: knowing where a metric's explanatory power stops is as valuable as the metric itself. The midfield index is a legitimate live, in-match diagnostic — it just isn't a crystal ball, and we can now say precisely what is instead of guessing.

Where the Ball Actually Goes Through Midfield

Every completed pass tagged with both the sender's and receiver's position group, across all 64 matches (50,259 passes) — showing how midfielders disperse the ball forward and collect it from behind, and which position group's total involvement (passing + defensive actions combined) actually rises when a team wins.

Dispersion — where a midfielder's pass goes
Collection — where a midfielder's received pass came from
Midfielders recycle more than they launch attacks directly — 51.7% of their passes go backward to defenders, only 18.9% go forward to forwards. They collect the ball the same way: 52.9% comes from defenders, just 16.3% from forwards. The "link" role is real, but it's asymmetric — far more defense-to-midfield traffic than midfield-to-attack.
Who does the highest share of the work when a team wins?
Defenders always have the largest raw share of total ball actions (42-46%) regardless of result — that's a function of build-up play starting deep, not a sign of importance.
The share that actually moves with winning is forwards, proportionally the most — forward involvement rises from 16.4% in losses to 18.5% in wins (+2.1pp, a ~13% relative jump), the largest proportional shift of any group. Midfielder share rises too (32.9% → 34.4%), a smaller but consistent move in the same direction. Defender share falls as teams win (45.7% → 42.4%).
Direct answer to the question: goals are scored by forwards, and forwards also show the sharpest proportional rise in overall involvement when teams win — not just midfielders. Midfielders contribute a steady, larger absolute share, but the metric that shifts most with winning is forward involvement, not midfield involvement. Both matter; forwards move more.
